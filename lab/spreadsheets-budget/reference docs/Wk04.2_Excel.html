What is a Spreadsheet?

Open Excel and open this file

Spreadsheets could be called number processing software as opposed to Word, which is word processing software.  All spreadsheet software (including Excel, Lotus and QuattroPro) is based on a table format – numbers (and text) are entered into cells, which are organized into rows and columns.  A spreadsheet can be used to:

  • Format data or calculations into an attractive table;
  • Perform complicated or repetitive calculations;
  • Analyze empirical (laboratory) data and determine the equation that best fits the data;
  • Graph data in many types of graphs;and
  • Store data in a database (although Excel is not as versatile or powerful as the Microsoft Access database program).

Examples:

A spreadsheet would be suitable for the following applications:

  • Calculating your tax return;
  • Preparing a cost estimate for a construction project;
  • Recording and calculating your marks on tests and assignments and then calculating each students final grade;
  • Estimating the flow rates in a drainage or sewerage analysis and calculating what pipe diameter would be required to pass the flow;
  • Calculating mortgage payments given the principal, interest rate and number of payments, calculating the balance owing after each payment;
  • To count the number of days / minutes / seconds until the year 2010 (or any other date)
  • Calculating the amount in an investment account each month for 20 years;
  • Calculating and graphing stress and strain and graphing the results in a lab; and
  • Performing statistical analysis (such as calculating the standard deviation) of a large number of data points.

Spreadsheet Lingo

At the end of this section in the course you should understand the following words:

Worksheet, Workbook, Tabs

A Worksheet is a single table within a Workbook; using several Worksheets makes it easier to organize different types of data; A Workbook is saved as a single .xlsx (.xls in older versions) file and all Worksheets within the Workbook are opened when you open the file.
 Worksheets are separated by Tabs visible at the bottom of the screen; Tabs are initially called Sheet1, Sheet2, Sheet3 but you can rename them by double clicking on the Tab.  You can also add more sheets (up to 256) if you need them by clicking the blank tab to the right of the existing tabs.

Chart

A Chart is a graph, a Chart can appear right on your table, called an Embedded Chart or it can have its own Tab which Excel will call Chart1, Chart2 but these can also be changed.

Column, Row, Cell

Columns are labeled with letters; Rows are labeled with numbers; Cells are identified by their column and row, for example:  A1, B265, G4, AA123 all the way to XFD1048576; these notations are called the cell’s address. There are in the neigbourhood of 17 billion cells.

Range

A Range is the name given to a collection of cells.  Ranges are identified by the address of two cells in diagonally opposite corners.  A colon separates the 2 addresses.  D5:A1, D1:A5 and A5:D1 are considered to be identical ranges.
Ranges can be typed or swiped with the mouse.
A range is used when we are using a formula as in the question “what is the maximum number found in the range B10:B40?” this is written in Excel as =MAX(B10:B40)

Formula and Formula Bar

When you want to do any sort of math or any operation, you write a formula in the cell where you want the answer to be. Formulae often point to other cells for their input data. For example, you might want to add the contents of a list of numbers and place the answer at the bottom. The formula would go at the bottom and would refer to all the cells above. For example:

=(A1+A2)*B3

A Formula is a mathematical operation; a cell can contain a formula that adds, subtracts, multiplies, divides and raises to a power numbers in any combinations of other cells.  The formula for the current cell will appear in the formula bar at the top of the screen.  The result of the formula will appear in the cell containing the formula.

Function

A Function is a tool provided by Excel to perform more complicated math functions than + - * / and ^.  There are hundreds of functions and many perform more than just math.  Functions also appear in the formula bar.
Examples of functions are:
=SUM(C3:C6) – totals the numbers in range C3:C6, if any text appears in those cells the text is ignored
=SUM(C3,C6) – totals the numbers in cells C3 and C6, note the slight difference in syntax
=AVERAGE(C3:C6) – calculates the average of the numbers in range C3:C6
=MAX(C3:C6) – gives the value of the largest number in the range C3:C6
=RAND() – creates a random number between 0 and 1.
=RAND()*100 - creates a random number between 0 and 100.
=TODAY() – gives the present date
=NOW() – gives the present date and time
=PI() - gives the value of pi to 10 significant figures

Functions can be part of a formula or embedded in other functions:
=TODAY()+7 – gives the date for 1 week from today.
=ABS(C8)+D1*3 – adds the absolute value of cell C8 to 3 times cell D1
=MAX(SUM(C1:C5),SUM(D1:D5)) – gives the value of whichever is greater of the two sums

The Function Wizard is very helpful for creating functions.  We will learn many more functions and their applications later.

Cell number formatting

Excel can understand the value of a number but display it in many different ways:  percent, commas, currency, any number of decimal places, fractions, dates (explained below), and scientific.  For Example the number 15.75 can be entered and formatted to appear as

January 15, 1900 6:00 PM (using the info for both date and time)
or 1.555E +1 (that is how a computer indicates 1.555x101)

Date Codes

 

In general, computers cannot understand dates and times unless they are converted to a strictly numerical value. Some systems count time as the # of seconds elapsed since January 1,1970; Windows counts the # of 1/10th of a milliseconds elapsed since January 1, 1601.

In Excel, the value is the elapsed time in days since midnight, January 1, 1900.  January 19, 2009 is date code 39832.  The time of day is in decimal days (0.5 is noon, 0.75 is 6:00 p.m.).

These numbers are formatted to appear on the screen as a date such as October 3, 2008 or 10/3/2008.  There are many options for how the date is displayed but they remain as numerical values.

Note that you can type a day as text and Excel will translate it to a date code and format it as a date so you may never even notice that is doing this.

Entities within Excel

A cell can contain:

  • Nothing (empty)
  • Text – will be either ignored, given a value of zero or give a error if you refer to a cell with text in a formula
  • Comment – these are like little Post-its assigned to a cell, a paragraph will pop up if you mouse over the cell
  • Number – numbers can be used in formulae, Excel will only perform math operations on numbers (remember dates are numbers)
    • Single number – a cell containing just a value
    • Formula or Function with a numerical value as output – discussed above

These items can appear on the Worksheet but are not assigned to a single cell:

  • Graphs  - Excel calls them Charts, graphs can either be on their own tab or next to your table on the same Worksheet – this is called and Embedded Chart.
  • Graphics – you can insert photos and clip art just like in Word
  • Equations – the Equation Editor is the same as that in Word

Copying and Moving

 

Standard Methods

The usual ways of copying and moving in Windows programs apply to Excel:

  • on the Ribbon

  • right-click with the mouse:
  • or with the keyboard:
    • Copy - Control-C
    • Paste - Control-V
    • Cut - Control-X (Move = Cut followed by Paste)

However, with Excel we can also drag to Move or Copy:

Moving:

  • click the cell F3 with the date; the cell will now have a bold outline indicating that it is the current cell
  • move your mouse to be on the bold outline (but not the bottom right corner of the outline)
  • your mouse will change from a big white plus-sign to a set of 4 arrows pointing outward (not possible to create a screen shot, sorry)
  • stay away from the small dark square at the bottom right corner; drag the bold outline of the cell so the date moves to another cell
    • Side Comment: If you have moved the date to a column narrower than column F then the column is not wide enough to display the date and will show ######. Do not worry. Widen the column by moving your mouse to the top where the columns are labeled A-Z. As you move to the right edge of a column your mouse will change to a vertical line with arrows pointing side to side. Drag the column wider or double-click at this point and the column will expand to fit the widest thing in that column.

Copying

If we want to copy a cell downward or sideways we can do this:

  • move your mouse to be on the bold outline at the bottom right corner of the outline there is a small dark square
  • your mouse will change to a small dark plus sign
  • drag that square downward or sideways - you cannot to both at the same time

Using Copy to Count

  • Put your mouse in the middle of cell A9 and drag to select the range A9:A10. The contents of those cells are a 2 and a 3.
  • We want to continue the counting to be 4,5,6,...
  • With 2 cells selected, if we drag the small dark square at the bottom right corner of the range, Excel will continue the count using the 2 selected numbers as the starting point.
  • Comments:
    • Excel doesn't necessarily have to count by 1s. If the cells contained 5 and 10, Excel would continue with 15, 20, 25, 30 etc.
    • If we did the same steps but selected only the 3 in cell A10 then Excel would not count; it would copy to create 3,3,3,3,3...
    • Excel responds differently when we copy down a cell where the contents of the cell starts with letters but ends with a number - such as if the cell contained 'trial #1.' Try it, type that in just one cell and copy it down. If we don't want to cell to change to read 'trial #2' we can use regular old Copy and Paste rather than dragging to copy.

What happens to cell references when you Move or Copy

Copying

  • Go to cell B9 and note the contents of the cell: '=A8/$C$5'
  • copy the $1.07 to the cell below
  • now look at the contents and note the changes
  • Comments:
    • cell references in Excel are either relative references or absolute references. The formula in cell B9 could be stated as "the cell to the left divided by cell C5." A8 is a relative reference; $C$5 is an absolute reference. The $ signs in front of the letter and the number lock the reference to that particular cell.
    • The copied formula is now '=A9/$C$5'; only the row of the relative reference has changed.
    • When we copy a formula one cell downward the relative references change to refer to the cell one lower.
    • When we copy a formula the absolute references do not change.
    • To add in the $ signs, either type them or hit the F4 key while editing the formula.
    • Also, it is possible to lock only the letter or the number. When copying only downward, only the number (row reference) needs locking. Similarly, with sideways copying, only the letter (column reference) needs locking. It generally doesn't matter if you lock both.
  • return to cell B9 and edit the formula to remove the $ signs. Copy it down a few rows and see how it's wrong.

Moving

  • When you Move a formula the cell references don't change.

Required format for all tables

On Each Printed Page and Each Tab in Excel:

All of your Excel tables should contain, at minimum, the following information at the top:

  • Title of Table
  • Your Name
  • The course number
  • The current date (use either the =TODAY() or =NOW() formulae to always display the current date or the =DATE(yyyy,mm,dd) formula to fix the date to a specified day)

Additional information you may want to include in other tables, perhaps in a print footer, are:

  • Camosun College and/or Civil Engineering Technology if there is a chance anyone other than you and your instructor may be viewing the table
  • page number, for multi page tables, and the total number of pages
  • the xlsx file name, so you can find it later; Excel can automatically generate file name and path
  • the current time, if you may be printing many in a day (use the =NOW() formula and format for time)

For Each Value / Column of values:

It should always be absolutely clear, on the computer and in a printed copy, what each number on a table represents, what its units are and how it relates to the other numbers on the table. To help this follow these rules:

  • if there are parameters that do not require a whole column, put them at the top of the table and give their units. Examples of these are:
    • initial settings (initial velocity, start height);
    • settings that will not change (mass of object, g, etc); or
    • settings that may change but will apply to the whole table (interest rate)
  • always put a title at the top of each column of numbers
  • always put the units below each title; convention requires us to put the unit in brackets and center it
