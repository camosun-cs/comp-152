Task 1: Finding Sources

This task will be completed by your group as a team. How you share the effort is up to you. However, every member is responsible for the end result, and all members of your group will receive the same mark.

FAQ on source finding

The Camosun library has many resources to help you with your research, including a list of Frequently Asked Questions about finding sources on the web.

Your team has been assigned a number of questions from an earlier class activity.

For each of your assigned questions:

  1. Find three public websites that address the question, and record the following details for each on the wiki:

    • a permanent URL
    • the date you accessed the site
    • the date the content was written
    • the name of the author

    If you cannot find any of the above details, choose a different resource! Missing dates and authors are a strong indicator of unreliable content.

    Permanent website URLs and dates

    Content on the web is constantly changing. Some pages may even change from one minute to the next! To help when referencing content, many websites offer "permanent" addresses (also known as "permalinks") to individual articles which are less likely to change significantly over time.

    Some kinds of pages are so variable over time that they are never useful as research material. This especially includes results pages from search engines, and almost anything on social media sites.

    Even "permanent" pages aren't really permanent. Authors on the web are free to make changes at any time, or may simply not have the resources to ensure that their content stays online. However, if you also record the date that you accessed something online, you'll have a better chance of finding the content you actually used when looking for it later.

    Some sites (such as wikis) record a log of changes over time, making it possible to see what has changed since you last visited, or even see what the content looked like at an earlier date. For sites that don't offer this feature, the Wayback Machine can often provide a similar service.

    Website authors

    The name of the person responsible for the content is very important when assessing the credibility and biases of the information presented on a website. Avoid referencing content that does not have the real full name of a person attached to it.

    It is rarely appropriate to reference material that is only attributable to a company, website, or other organization. The main exceptions are official press-release materials or documents produced by committee or government. Other resources are often given much less editorial attention, have biases that are much harder to identify, and may not even be fully endorsed by the publishing organization.

    Websites with a large number of authors who aren't part of a formal committee (e.g. Wikipedia) aren't usually good resources for academic research, since their credibility is diluted by the least trust-worthy contributor. However, they are often useful for finding other resources which would make good sources by following their references!

  2. Find a book or periodical available from the Camosun library that addresses the question and record a permanent library catalog URL or full citation on your wiki page. Ensure that the full text is available online or can be physically checked out for your use during this assignment.

    Permanent library URLs

    Because access to the Camosun library catalog is restricted by a log-in system, the URLs of pages you visit shown by your web browser are specific to you and will not be functional for other users. To work around this, the library catalog provides a "Permalink" feature on all catalog pages.

    Always use the URL provided by the this feature when creating citations or otherwise sharing a source with other people!

  3. Find an informative graphic or video related to the question. On the wiki, record:

    • a permanent URL of the context in which the media was found
    • the date you accessed the resource
    • the date the resource was created
    • the name of the author and copyright holder

    If you cannot find any of the above details, choose a different resource! Copyright information (date and name) is particularly important when referencing media that you do not own.

  4. If possible, download a copy of the media from the previous step and attach it to your wiki page.

    Copies and copyright

    It is usually permissible under Canadian "fair dealing" laws to make use of copyrighted material for the purpose of study in an academic setting. However, these exemptions do not give you the right to claim copyrighted works as your own! It is very important to properly attribute any media you directly include or link to in your work to the real author and copyright holders.

  5. For each of the research materials found above, answer these three questions:

    1. How did the author collect the information for this resource? (e.g. interviews, original research, personal experience, cited sources)
    2. What is the author's agenda? (i.e. why did they create this resource? Are they trying to convince you, warn you, hype something, sell something, etc?)
    3. What other biases might the author have about this material? (i.e. are they motivated to present the information in a particular light?)

An example of the expected results for Task 1 is available on the wiki.

Task 2: Evaluating Sources

Each member will be marked individually for their work in this task.

  1. Read the sources your team has found and consider how useful each is in answering your research questions.

    Questions for evaluating sources
    • Is this relevant and significant for the question at hand?
    • Does this add any new information?
    • Are there other aspects to your question that this source may have missed?
    • Is the information up to date? Are there newer resources available?
    • Is this information corroborated by another source?
    • Do other credible sources give conflicting opinions?
    • Is this resource peer-reviewed?
    • Is the author an expert and/or are their sources reliable?
    • How could the place or time this was written have affected its credibility?
    • Is the author being paid to write this? By whom?
    • Does the author's agenda overly bias the information?
    • Are opinions or subjective information being presented as facts?
    • Is the research methodology repeatable?
    • Does this resource present a false balance?
  2. Choose two sources that you think are the most valuable. Create a discussion topic on your group's wiki page for each and describe what you think makes them so good. Use some of the "Questions for evaluating sources" above to support your reasoning.
  3. Choose two sources that you think are the least useful. Create a discussion topic on your group's wiki page for each and describe what you think makes them less valuable. Use some of the "Questions for evaluating sources" above to support your reasoning.

If several group members agree that a particular source is of poor quality, you should probably go back to Task 1 and find a replacement!
